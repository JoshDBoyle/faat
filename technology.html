The Foundation for Applied Aviation Technology, Inc. (FAAT) was founded by a group of supply chain managers and engineers who know they can make a difference in aviation. First, the founding Group recognized the disconnect between the knowledge acquired by engineering students in the academic setting and the practical knowledge needed to be effective in the design/manufacturing environment. Presently, academia attempts to provide practical experience through (1) coop and/or intern programs for students and (2) competitive participation programs with other Universities. The Group knows that more can be done to assist engineering students in the transition between knowledge acquisition and knowledge application by establishing Airframe & Powerplant (A&P) licensing programs. An A&P license is recognition of having acquired the practical knowledge for aircraft maintenance. The Group wants to establish A&P licensing programs for engineering students at Universities as a credential to be acquired through qualified training programs. An A&P License is the penultimate credential in the aerospace engineering field (the Designated Engineering Representative [DER] is the ultimate). Second, the founding Group recognized that certain technologies are not being utilized in the General Aviation (GA) market because of institutional limitations. In most cases, unless there is a patent or controlled proprietary process involved in the advancement of a technology, the development funding from the venture capital market would not be forthcoming. This is understandable since the venture capital’s investment cannot be protected for maximum duration if there is no patent or protected proprietary process. What this means for certain technologies is that they won’t get developed or implemented even though such advancements would add to the safety, efficiency, positive environmental impact of aircraft systems. Furthermore, it appears as though advancements in aircraft systems are extremely slow to develop because of the associated perceived risks to the corporate sponsors. As in other industries, major advancements in technology were not made by the “main stream” corporate players, but were accomplished by the smaller, innovative, outside firms. In light of the above stated institutional limitations, the founding Group will fund the advancement of General Aviation technologies on a “per project” basis. Ultimately, the founding group’s objective is to combine the development of technology function with the education function thereby allowing engineering students to participate in the process of transferring the practical technology to aviation.
